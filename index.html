Robert Jagiello

I am a researcher in social psychology and evolutionary anthropology interested in the motivations that guide belief formation, social learning and cultural transmission. I completed my PhD at the University of Oxford under the supervision of Prof Harvey Whitehouse, based at the Centre for the Study of Social Cohesion (CSSC) .

I am equally interested in supernatural and conspiratorial belief systems, the social cognition underlying moral norms and the evolution of morality as a cooperative device.

My work integrates experimental psychology, cultural evolutionary theory and computational approaches, with earlier training in cognitive neuroscience using EEG and pupillometry.

Research

Working Papers

Magic and ritual as affiliative strategies in response to ostracism threat

Robert Jagiello & Harvey Whitehouse

Submitted and in review

The transmission of rituals and magical practices is a fundamental feature of human culture, yet the psychological mechanisms underpinning their persistence remain contested. The Bifocal Stance Theory (BST) posits that individuals adopt either a ritual stance, which prioritizes social affiliation and norm adherence, or an instrumental stance, which emphasizes efficiency in achieving goal-directed outcomes. Using an online adaptation of the pen-and-cups paradigm, we show that ostracized individuals copy causally opaque actions with higher fidelity, supporting the view that ritualized and magical actions serve affiliative rather than instrumental functions.

Ostracism and the Spread of Natural and Supernatural Concepts

Robert Jagiello & Harvey Whitehouse

Submitted and in review

We present experimental evidence that supernatural concepts are more readily interpreted through a ritual stance than an instrumental stance when learned in social contexts, particularly under conditions of social exclusion. These findings have implications for the evolutionary origins of religion.

Robert Jagiello, Cecilia Heyes , & Harvey Whitehouse

Behavioral and Brain Sciences (2022)

The bifocal stance theory explains how innovation and tradition coexist in cultural evolution by allowing learners to flexibly adopt instrumental or ritual motivations during social learning.
